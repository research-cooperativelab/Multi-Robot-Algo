\chapter{EXPERIMENTAL EVALUATION}\label{ch:results}

\begin{doublespacing}

This chapter reports the Monte Carlo experiments that test the four
research hypotheses of Chapter~III and verify the theoretical bounds
of Chapter~IV.

\section{Experimental Setup}

All experiments are conducted on a common instance generator. Site
positions are drawn uniformly at random in the square $[0, L]^2$ with
$L = 10$. Prior probabilities are drawn from
$\mathrm{Uniform}(0.1, 1.0)$ and normalized to sum to one. Robot bases
are drawn uniformly at random in the same square. The target site is
then drawn according to the normalized prior, subject to two filters:
the optimal distance must satisfy $d_{\mathrm{opt}} \geq 1.0$ to avoid
degenerate ratios in which the target happens to lie essentially on
top of a base, and the reachability constraint
$2\,d_{\mathrm{opt}} \leq E$ is imposed to ensure that at least one
robot can reach the target within its energy budget. The default
parameters are $n = 30$ sites, $R = 3$ robots, and $E = 14$ units of
energy; 5,000 trials are run on identical instances across all models to
enable paired comparisons.

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports the main comparison. Each row gives the
mean finder competitive ratio, the median FCR, the standard deviation,
and the average number of iterations until the target is found.

\emph{Hypothesis H1 (communication dominates fleet size).} The
ratio of M1's FCR (18.74) to M2's (2.83) is 6.6, confirming that
auction-based coordination alone produces a large improvement. The
difference is not a matter of statistical noise---with 5,000 trials
and the reported standard deviations, the two means are separated by
many standard errors (Wilcoxon $p = 2.9\times10^{-153}$). H1 is supported.

\emph{Significance.} The M1--M2 comparison isolates the effect of
communication: both models share the same fleet size, sites, priors,
and targets---the sole difference is whether robots exchange
information.
A $6.6\times$ improvement attributable entirely to coordination
confirms \citet{Chrobak2015}'s theoretical result that the
\emph{mode} of communication is a more impactful design lever than
fleet size: equipping a three-robot team with coordination produces
larger gains than adding robots to an uncoordinated team.
In practical terms, a reliable communication link in a rubble
environment contributes more to search efficiency than additional
vehicles.
Figure~\ref{fig:m1-vs-m4star} illustrates this on a single instance:
the uncoordinated M1 team repeatedly revisits the same regions and
achieves FCR $53.65$, while M4* on the same instance completes the
search at FCR $3.02$ with compact, non-overlapping tours (single-instance
example; the five-thousand-trial mean for M4* is $3.11 \pm 0.058$).

\emph{Hypothesis H2 (probability-aware bids beat distance-only
centralized assignment).} M3 (FCR $= 6.32$) outperforms the
distance-only Hungarian baseline H$_d$ (FCR $= 6.88$) by 8.1 percent
(Wilcoxon $p = 3.4\times10^{-15}$), supporting H2 directly.
The follow-up question of whether the gap is attributable to the
\emph{bid function} (probability-weighted vs.\ distance-only) or to
the \emph{allocation mechanism} (distributed SSI vs.\ centralized
Hungarian) was tested via a probability-weighted Hungarian ablation
H$_{p/d}$ (Table~\ref{tab:main}).
Across 5,000 trials, H$_{p/d}$ achieves a mean FCR of $7.38$---
\emph{worse} than both H$_d$ ($6.88$) and M3 ($6.32$).
This reversal is explained by what can be called a \emph{clustering pathology}
in the centralized Hungarian assignment.
The Hungarian solver with $p/d$ costs assigns all robots simultaneously from
their fixed bases at the start of each round, using the same probability map.
Because all robots see the same high-probability sites and are assigned from
fixed starting points, they tend to cluster toward the same regions of the
arena each round, leaving low-probability areas systematically uncovered.
In contrast, the SSI auction removes each won site from competition
\emph{before} the next robot bids, so its iterative re-weighting
naturally prevents robots from clustering on the same high-probability sites.
The distributed sequential nature of the SSI mechanism is therefore the
essential ingredient driving the improvement over H$_d$; probability-weighted
costs alone, applied to a one-shot centralized assignment, are not only
insufficient but counterproductive at scale. H2 is supported.

\emph{Hypothesis H3 (multi-site sorties recover most of the energy
penalty).} The gap M3 $-$ M2 $= 6.32 - 2.83 = 3.49$ in FCR is the
energy penalty under single-sortie constraints. Model 4's FCR of
$3.16$ recovers
$(6.32 - 3.16)/(6.32 - 2.83) = 90\%$ of that gap (Wilcoxon
$p = 3.2\times10^{-107}$). The mechanism is
visible in the iteration count: M3 takes $4.81$ iterations on average
while M4 takes $1.38$, because each M4 sortie visits approximately
$5.3$ sites.

\emph{Significance.} The M2--M3 gap quantifies the performance cost
of the energy constraint relative to an unconstrained baseline.
The $90\%$ recovery achieved by M4 demonstrates that this cost is
largely not a hardware limitation but an algorithmic one: most of
the energy penalty is recoverable through multi-site sortie planning
rather than requiring higher-capacity batteries.
Robot teams operating under battery constraints can recover the
majority of the energy penalty through improved planning alone.
H3 is supported.

\emph{Hypothesis H4 ($p/d^2$ is empirically optimal).} Model 4*
achieves a mean FCR of $3.11$ versus Model 4's $3.16$---a directional
improvement of $1.5$ percent in the correct direction. As reported in
Table~\ref{tab:main} footnote~(d), this difference is \emph{not}
statistically significant at the default configuration (Wilcoxon $W = 119{,}002$,
$p = 0.88$, Bonferroni threshold $\alpha/4 = 0.0125$).
Figure~\ref{fig:bids} compares all bid functions studied and shows the
ranking $p/d^2 > p/d > 1/d > p\,e^{-d/E} > p$ in the single-sortie
bid sweep (Model 3); the distance-only bid $1/d$ produces an FCR of
approximately $6.79$, consistent with the Hungarian baseline H$_d$ at
$6.88$---a cross-implementation consistency check.
The cost-foreclosure argument of Section~\ref{sec:foreclosure} predicts
that the quadratic distance penalty should be most effective when energy
is scarce relative to inter-site spacing. This prediction is validated
by the energy sweep (Figure~\ref{fig:energy}), where the $p/d^2$
advantage over $p/d$ grows as $E$ decreases, and by the instance
scaling sweep (Section~\ref{sec:instance-scaling}), where M4* surpasses
M2 at large fleet sizes. H4 is supported by the energy and bid-function
sweeps and by the theoretical cost-foreclosure argument; the aggregate
effect at the default configuration ($n = 30$, $R = 3$, $E = 14$) falls
below the detection threshold at 1,000 trials.

\emph{Significance.} The substitution of $p/d$ by $p/d^2$ requires
a single modification to the bid formula.
The cost-foreclosure mechanism is general: it applies wherever an
agent's budget for follow-up actions is reduced by its initial
commitment, such that anchoring farther away forecloses the budget
available for chain extensions.
The energy sweep confirms that the benefit grows when $E$ is tight
relative to the inter-site spacing.
For multi-robot deployments with constrained energy budgets, the
recommendation is to substitute $d^2$ for $d$ in the bid
denominator; the advantage materializes in the energy-constrained
regime.

All four bounds hold across 500 trials at the default configuration (Table~\ref{tab:bounds}). The
Model 2 bound is the tightest at $0.85$: the model has the simplest
cost structure, one hop per round, and the bound reflects this
accurately. The Model 4 bound is the loosest because the analytical
$S$ (number of sites per sortie) is an underestimate: the theoretical
$S$ is computed from the average inter-point distance $d_{\mathrm{hop}}$,
but the greedy extension preferentially selects sites that happen to
be \emph{closer} than $d_{\mathrm{hop}}$, so the empirical average is
about $5.3$ sites per sortie at $E = 14$. Tightening the Model 4 bound
via expected-greedy-chain-length analysis is a direction for future
work, discussed in Chapter~VI.

\section{Parameter Sweeps}

\subsection{Energy Sweep}\label{sec:energy}

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{fig_energy_sweep.png}
  \caption{FCR as a function of energy budget $E$ at $R = 3$. Model 4* converges toward and eventually overtakes Model 2; Model 3 and the Hungarian baseline plateau because single-node sorties cannot exploit additional energy.}\label{fig:energy}
\end{figure}

As the energy budget $E$ is swept from $8$ to $30$ (Figure~\ref{fig:energy}),
Model 4*'s FCR decreases from $3.18$ to $2.67$, eventually surpassing
Model 2 ($2.83$). This crossover does not contradict the framing of
Model 2 as a strong baseline. Model 2 is not an algorithmic lower bound.
Its greedy node-to-node auction can commit to a sub-optimal chain
whereas Model 4* re-plans anchor sites at the base at the start of
every iteration using the quadratic-penalty bid. With enough energy,
the benefit of periodic replanning outweighs the overhead of returning
to base. Model 3 and the Hungarian baseline H$_d$ both plateau near
$6.5$--$7.0$ regardless of $E$, because a single-node sortie simply
cannot convert surplus energy into additional site visits.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{On the $d_{\mathrm{opt}}$ Filters}

Two filters were applied during instance generation. The first,
$d_{\mathrm{opt}} \geq 1.0$, excludes instances in which the target
happens to lie nearly on top of a base and the optimal travel cost is
essentially zero. Without the filter, FCR explodes regardless of
algorithm quality, and the signal being measured becomes a function
of instance geometry rather than algorithmic choice. This filter
removes roughly 7 percent of generated instances, and it introduces
a well-defined \emph{selection bias}: the reported averages describe
performance on instances where some algorithmic choice matters, not
on the full generated distribution. Two steps mitigate the bias.
First, the median FCR is reported in Table~\ref{tab:main} alongside
the mean to characterize the distribution without the influence of
the remaining right tail; the medians and means support the same
ranking. Second, a full sensitivity sweep over
$d_{\mathrm{opt}} \in \{0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$ was run at 500 trials
per threshold. The mean FCRs are reported below:

The relative ranking
M2 $<$ M4* $<$ M4 $<$ M3 $<$ H$_d$ $<$ M1 is preserved at every
threshold; only the absolute FCRs shift. M1's mean shrinks as the
threshold increases because removing trivially-close targets reduces
the right tail of the FCR distribution; the ratio-based models
M3 and H$_d$ instead rise as the threshold increases, because the
average $d_{\mathrm{opt}}$ in the denominator grows more slowly than
the finder's distance on harder instances. The
second filter, $2\,d_{\mathrm{opt}} \leq E$, excludes instances that
no single-sortie algorithm could ever solve and that would therefore
be unfairly penalized. In a real deployment, a target trivially
close to a base is an easy case that no algorithm needs to optimize
for; excluding it concentrates the evaluation on the instances where
algorithmic choice actually matters.

\subsection{On the Choice of Greedy Chain Extension}

The chain extension in Model 4 / 4* is greedy and $O(n)$ per hop.
More sophisticated combinatorial tour optimizers---2-opt local search
or Christofides' algorithm---could in principle produce shorter chains.
Two reasons motivate the greedy choice in this thesis. First, the
thesis targets real-time planning: each robot must decide its route
before departing and cannot afford the quadratic or super-quadratic
time of combinatorial methods. Second, the greedy chain is equivalent
to Smith's WSPT scheduling rule~\citep{Smith1956} and is empirically
within $7.7$ percent of brute-force optimal ordering across 500
trials---a gap small enough that the algorithm's overall performance
is not dominated by chain sub-optimality. A 2-opt post-processing pass on M4* at the default config (1,000 trials)
actually \emph{increases} mean FCR from $3.093$ to $3.257$
(a $+5.3$ percent degradation relative to the greedy chain). The
mechanism is that 2-opt minimizes \emph{total} tour length, which
tends to reorder sites so that the high-probability target site is
visited later in the tour, inflating the finder's distance. This
confirms that the finder-centric FCR rewards visiting
high-probability sites \emph{early}---the exact behavior the greedy
probability-weighted chain is designed to produce. A tour optimizer
that minimizes expected FCR rather than total tour length would need
to account for the probability distribution over target locations,
a direction discussed in Chapter~VI.

\subsection{On When the Quadratic Penalty Is Less Suitable}

The $p/d^2$ bid is motivated by a cost-foreclosure argument that
applies when energy is scarce relative to inter-site distance. At the
low-energy end of the sweep ($E = 8$), the gap between Model 4 and
Model 4* narrows to $3$ percent, because all feasible tours are short
regardless of bid function. In the limit of abundant energy, the same
is true from the other direction: the chain-foreclosure effect that
motivates the quadratic penalty diminishes, and the standard $p/d$
bid performs comparably. The $p/d^2$ bid is therefore most useful in
the intermediate regime that corresponds to typical real-world
deployments.

\subsection{Simplifying Assumptions}

The model assumes perfect sensors, flat Euclidean terrain, and a
uniform energy budget across robots. The perfect-sensor assumption
is the most consequential of these: in a real collapsed-structure
deployment, dust, occlusion, and rubble produce \emph{false
negatives}, and a robot can leave a site where the target is present
and report ``not here.'' A false negative corrupts the Bayesian
renormalization of Chapter~III because the update sets $p_i
\leftarrow 0$ for a visited site that should still carry residual
mass, so the posterior over-concentrates on the unvisited sites and
the true target may be permanently excluded. The remedy is
straightforward: for a known detection probability $p_{\mathrm{det}}
< 1$ the update becomes $p_i \leftarrow (1 - p_{\mathrm{det}}) p_i$
rather than zero, leaving residual mass proportional to the miss
rate. Expected rounds then scale by roughly $1/p_{\mathrm{det}}$, and
the bounds of Chapter~IV generalize by replacing $R$ with $R \cdot
p_{\mathrm{det}}$ in Lemma~\ref{lem:rounds}. A revisit policy
triggered when the posterior concentrates back toward a visited site
would recover any target missed on an earlier pass. For non-Euclidean
environments, Euclidean distances would be replaced by shortest-path
costs through an obstacle graph; the auction mechanism is agnostic
to how distances are computed. Heterogeneous energy budgets
introduce per-robot feasibility thresholds that complicate the
auction's candidate filtering but do not change the structure of the
algorithm.

\end{doublespacing}
