\section{Motivation}

Following a structural collapse---a building failure, an earthquake, a
mine cave-in---autonomous robot teams offer a critical operational
advantage over manual search: they can be deployed immediately into
unstable environments that are unsafe for human responders, they can
operate continuously without fatigue, and they can process sensor data
systematically rather than relying on hearing or visual clues alone.
In a typical urban search-and-rescue (USAR) deployment, a fleet of
aerial or ground robots is dispatched to survey $n$ candidate sites
derived from prior knowledge of the environment---structural surveys,
thermal imaging, acoustic sensors, or trained search-dog
indications---each assigned a \emph{prior probability} $p_i$ that the target
(a survivor, a critical sensor, a chemical source) is present there.

The operational challenge is not perception or locomotion; it is
\emph{coordination under resource constraints}.
Each robot departs from a charging base, carries a finite energy
supply, and can communicate with its teammates only upon returning to
recharge.
The team must collectively determine which robot visits which site,
and in what order, such that the first robot to locate the target has
traveled as short a distance as possible.
Two practical constraints make this non-trivial:

\emph{Finite energy:}  A robot that visits one site and returns to base
for recharging wastes most of its battery on travel overhead.
If it can chain several sites into one excursion, the same battery
covers far more of the search space---but chaining too aggressively
risks running out of charge mid-flight.

\emph{Communication at base only:}  In rubble-pile environments, radio
links are unreliable during flight.
Coordination must be achieved at the base station between sorties,
using a protocol that is fast enough not to delay deployments.

These two constraints interact: under tight \emph{energy budgets}, every
wasted sortie---one that revisits a cleared site or visits a
low-probability one---is a larger fraction of the total searchable
volume, so coordination quality matters \emph{more} as energy grows
scarcer.
Field analyses of multi-robot deployments at large-scale collapse
events~\citep{ACMSurveyMRTA2025} consistently identify duplicated
coverage and premature battery exhaustion as the dominant operational
failures.
The February 2023 earthquake in Turkey and Syria, which rendered more
than 160,000 structures uninhabitable and saw large-scale deployment
of autonomous systems for survivor localization, exemplifies this
pattern: robots duplicated coverage, radio links were unreliable in
the rubble, and battery life was the primary constraint.

\section{The Problem This Thesis Addresses}

This thesis studies the following formal problem.
A fleet of $R$ robots operates in a bounded two-dimensional environment
containing $n$ candidate sites, each with a prior probability $p_i$
of containing the target.
Robots depart from a base station, inspect sites (determining
definitively whether the target is present), and return to base.
Communication is restricted to the base.
The team has no prior map of the target's exact location---only the
probability distribution $\mathbf{p}$ over sites.

Performance is measured by the \emph{Finder Competitive Ratio (FCR)}:
the ratio of the total distance traveled by the robot that ultimately
locates the target to the minimum distance any robot could have
traveled had it known the target's location in advance.
Formally, if robot $f$ finds the target after traveling distance $D_f$,
and the nearest base-to-target distance is $D_{\mathrm{opt}}$, then
\[
  \mathrm{FCR} = D_f \,/\, D_{\mathrm{opt}}.
\]
When $\mathrm{FCR} = 1$, the discovering robot reached the target via
the shortest possible path from its base---the algorithm is
omniscience-equivalent for that instance.
Values greater than~1 quantify the multiplicative overhead introduced
by uncertainty and coordination constraints.
Lower FCR is better.
This metric is appropriate for SAR because it measures precisely the
quantity that matters operationally: how efficiently the \emph{finding}
robot expended its resource budget relative to the best achievable
outcome.

\section{Our Approach: A Hierarchy of Four Base Models}

Rather than proposing a single algorithm, this thesis introduces a
hierarchy of four base models that systematically isolate the
contribution of each operational capability.
At the base of the hierarchy is \textbf{Model~1 (M1)}, a
coordination-free baseline: robots independently sample sites from
the prior distribution, with no communication.
M1 quantifies the cost of zero coordination and provides a lower
bound on performance against which everything else is measured.

\textbf{Model~2 (M2)} adds full auction-based coordination and removes
the energy constraint, establishing an upper bound on what
communication alone can achieve.
In M2, robots share current position information, bid for sites using a
probability-weighted score, and are assigned sites via a \emph{sequential
single-item} (SSI) auction~\citep{Koenig2006}.
The gap between M1 and M2 measures the value of coordination.

\textbf{Model~3 (M3)} reintroduces the energy constraint while retaining
the auction protocol.
Each robot makes exactly one round trip per iteration, and the auction
selects only from energy-feasible sites.
The gap between M2 and M3 measures the cost of the finite battery.

\textbf{Model~4 and M4*} extend M3 by allowing each robot to chain
multiple sites into a single sortie before returning to base.
After the auction assigns an anchor site, a \emph{greedy chain extension}
appends additional sites while budget remains.
M4 uses the classical $p/d$ bid function; M4* uses a novel
$p/d^2$ bid function introduced and justified in this thesis.
The gap between M3 and M4 measures the recovery from intelligent
multi-node route planning.

These models are intentional abstractions, not engineering blueprints
for any specific platform.
They constitute a \emph{foundational framework} that isolates the
effect of each design decision under controlled, analytically
tractable conditions---and provides a reproducible benchmark
infrastructure on which future, more complex algorithms can be
evaluated.

\section{Principal Findings}

The four models yield four principal findings, each corresponding to
one of the research hypotheses stated in Chapter~\ref{ch:method}:

\emph{Coordination dominates fleet size:}  M2 (auction-coordinated,
$\mathrm{FCR} = 2.83$) outperforms M1 (uncoordinated,
$\mathrm{FCR} = 18.55$) by a factor of~$6.6$ at identical fleet size.
The mode of communication, not the number of robots, is the primary
determinant of search efficiency---consistent with the theoretical
bound of \citet{Chrobak2015}.

\emph{Probability-aware bids outperform distance-optimal centralized
assignment:}  M3 ($\mathrm{FCR} = 6.41$) outperforms the Hungarian
distance-optimal baseline ($\mathrm{FCR} = 6.80$) by $5.7\%$, despite
the Hungarian algorithm computing the globally optimal assignment each
iteration.
A probability-weighted Hungarian ablation narrows this gap to $1.4\%$,
establishing that the advantage is attributable to the bid function,
not to the distributed mechanism.

\emph{Multi-node sorties recover the energy penalty:}  M4
($\mathrm{FCR} = 3.11$) recovers $92\%$ of the performance gap between
M3 and M2 by chaining an average of $5.3$ sites per sortie, reducing
average iteration count from $4.81$ to~$1.38$.

\emph{The $p/d^2$ bid is superior under energy constraints:}  M4*
($\mathrm{FCR} = 2.98$) improves upon M4 by $4.2\%$.
The improvement is justified by a cost-foreclosure argument: a distant
anchor site not only costs more energy to reach, but also shortens the
remaining budget for chain extensions---making the effective marginal
cost of distance superlinear.
The $p/d^2$ bid captures this by penalizing distance quadratically.

All theoretical bounds were validated through 500 Monte Carlo trials
per configuration, with paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests confirming
statistical significance ($p < 0.001$, Bonferroni-corrected) for all
four comparisons.
Parameter sweeps over energy budget $E \in [8, 30]$ and fleet size
$R \in [1, 6]$ demonstrate the stability of these findings across
operating conditions.

\section{Scope and Contributions}

The scope of this thesis is algorithmic and simulation-based.
Physical robot deployment is left as future work
(Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}).

The specific contributions are:

\emph{A unified base framework of four models:}  This framework spans the
energy--communication design space, together with closed-form upper
bounds on the expected FCR for each.
It constitutes the first competitive-ratio analysis of budgeted
multi-robot SAR that integrates energy constraints, auction-based
coordination, and a probabilistic target model in a single framework.

\emph{An ablation isolating bid function from allocation mechanism:}  A
probability-weighted Hungarian baseline is introduced to separate the
effect of probability-aware bids from the effect of centralized
assignment; the bid function is identified as the dominant factor.

\emph{A novel $p/d^2$ bid function:}  The theoretical justification
proceeds via the cost-foreclosure argument, establishing that the
effective marginal cost of distance is superlinear under a fixed
per-sortie energy budget.
This is the primary algorithmic contribution.

\emph{A fully reproducible open-source benchmark:}  The
\textsc{SearchFCR} Python library, command-line interface, and
interactive web-based simulator enable direct replication and extension
of all results presented in this thesis.

\section{Key Definitions}

The following terms are used throughout the thesis.
Formal definitions appear in Chapter~\ref{ch:problem}.

A \emph{robot} is an autonomous mobile agent capable of traveling to a
site, performing an instantaneous inspection, and returning to a base
station; the results generalize beyond aerial vehicles to any mobile
agent class.

A \emph{site} is a candidate location $i \in \{1,\ldots,n\}$ with known
spatial position and prior probability $p_i$ of containing the target;
sites are fixed throughout each instance.

The \emph{prior probability} $p_i$ is the probability that site $i$
contains the target, derived from domain knowledge before search begins.
Priors sum to one ($\sum_i p_i = 1$), and after a negative inspection of
site $i$, the prior is Bayesian-updated to zero and the remaining
probabilities are renormalized.

A \emph{sortie} is a single round trip by one robot: base $\rightarrow$
one or more sites $\rightarrow$ base; energy-constrained sorties must
satisfy a budget $E$.

The coordination protocol used by Models~2--4 is a
\emph{Sequential Single-Item (SSI) auction}: one site is auctioned per
round, every robot submits a bid for every available site, the globally
highest bid wins and that (robot, site) pair is removed, and the process
repeats until all robots hold an assignment.

A \emph{bid function} maps a (robot, site) pair to a scalar bid.
Five functions are studied: $p_i$ (probability only), $1/d$
(distance only), $p_i/d$ (Stone's rule), $p_i/d^2$ (this thesis),
and $p_i e^{-d/E}$ (exponential budget decay).

The \emph{Finder Competitive Ratio (FCR)} is the ratio of the finding
robot's total travel distance $D_f$ to the omniscient optimum
$D_{\mathrm{opt}} = \min_r d(x_0^r, x_\tau)$.
$\mathrm{FCR} = 1$ is optimal; $\mathrm{FCR} = k$ means the finding
robot traveled $k$ times the minimum possible distance.

\section{Thesis Organization}

Chapter~\ref{ch:lit} reviews the three research traditions on which
this thesis builds: competitive search theory, market-based
multi-robot coordination, and energy-constrained probabilistic search.
It identifies the gap that motivates this work and positions the
present contributions relative to prior art.

Chapter~\ref{ch:problem} states the formal problem: the search instance
definition, the sortie model, the Finder Competitive Ratio, and the
evaluation baselines (Hungarian assignment variants).

Chapter~\ref{ch:method} presents the four algorithmic models with
pseudocode, the bid functions, the theoretical upper bounds on
expected FCR, and the implementation details including the
\textsc{SearchFCR} package and interactive simulator.

Chapter~\ref{ch:results} presents the experimental evaluation:
main comparison results with statistical significance tests,
theoretical bounds verification, and parameter sweeps over energy
and fleet size, followed by a discussion of filter sensitivity,
the Target-Delay Anomaly, and base-model assumptions.

Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion} summarizes the findings, identifies
directions for future work, and discusses the path toward physical
deployment.
