\textbf{ABSTRACT}

\vspace{0.25in}
\textbf{ON THE NUMERICAL ANALYSIS OF MULTI-ROBOT SEARCH\\
AND RESCUE ALGORITHMS IN UNKNOWN\\
ENVIRONMENTS}

\vspace{0.25in}
By

\vspace{0.1in}
Fozhan Babaeiyan Ghamsari

\vspace{0.1in}
Spring 2026
\end{center}

\begin{doublespacing}
\noindent
This thesis studies the multi-robot search and rescue problem in which
$R$ robots must locate a target hidden among $n$ candidate sites in a
two-dimensional environment. Each site carries a prior probability of
containing the target, and each robot operates from a base station with
a finite energy budget $E$. Four algorithmic models that systematically
vary energy capacity and coordination mechanism are formulated and
evaluated using the Finder Competitive Ratio (FCR), a metric that
measures how far the finding robot travels relative to an omniscient
optimum. For each model, theoretical upper bounds on the expected FCR
are derived and validated through 500 Monte Carlo trials with parameter
sweeps over energy budgets and fleet sizes. The results show that
auction-based coordination yields a 6.6-fold improvement over
uncoordinated search; that probability-aware distributed auctions
outperform a centralized distance-optimal (Hungarian) assignment by
5.7 percent, with the effect attributable to the bid function rather
than to centralization; that multi-node sorties recover 92 percent of
the gap between energy-constrained and unconstrained performance; and
that a bid function $p_i/d^2$ that penalizes distance quadratically
improves upon the standard $p_i/d$ by 4.2 percent under energy
constraints. The theoretical bounds hold across all trials, and the
experimental rankings are stable under variation in the filtering
thresholds used to exclude degenerate instances.
\end{doublespacing}
