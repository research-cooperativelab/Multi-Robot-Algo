ABSTRACT

\vspace{0.25in}
ON THE NUMERICAL ANALYSIS OF MULTI-ROBOT SEARCH\\
AND RESCUE ALGORITHMS IN UNKNOWN\\
ENVIRONMENTS

\vspace{0.25in}
By

\vspace{0.1in}

\vspace{0.1in}

\begin{doublespacing}
\noindent
Every minute after a structural collapse, the probability of finding
survivors alive decreases.
This thesis studies the multi-robot search and rescue problem in which
$R$ robots must locate a target among $n$ candidate sites, each with a
prior probability $p_i$, under a finite per-robot energy budget $E$.
Four algorithmic models are introduced: M1, an uncoordinated random
baseline with no inter-robot communication; M2, a
\emph{Sequential Single-Item (SSI) auction} with unconstrained energy;
M3, an energy-constrained auction with a $p_i/d$ bid; and M4/M4*,
which chains sites per sortie via a \emph{greedy chain extension} using
the novel $p_i/d^2$ bid, alongside a \emph{Hungarian assignment}
baseline.
Performance is measured by the \emph{Finder Competitive Ratio} (FCR),
validated through 5,000 Monte Carlo trials.
Four results emerge: auction coordination yields a $6.6\times$ FCR
improvement over random search; \emph{Sequential Single-Item} auctions
outperform centralized Hungarian assignment by $8.1\%$; multi-node
sorties recover $90\%$ of the energy penalty; and $p_i/d^2$ achieves
the best FCR under energy constraints, with advantage growing to
${\sim}12\%$ at tight budgets.
Therefore, this thesis provides a reproducible foundational benchmark
applicable to any scenario in which resource-constrained agents must
locate a target under uncertainty, from urban search-and-rescue to
planetary exploration.
\end{doublespacing}
